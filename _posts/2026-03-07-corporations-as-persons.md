## Introduction

Corporations are legal persons. They hold rights—to contract, to own property, to speak, and under *Citizens United* to spend on political campaigns. They are sued and regulated as persons. When they cause harm, we expect them to bear civil and sometimes administrative liability. But when it comes to criminal responsibility, the same legal persons are often treated with less seriousness than natural persons who cause comparable harm. This essay argues for symmetry: if a corporation is a person for rights and civil purposes, it should be a person for criminal purposes too—investigated, charged, and punished with seriousness that fits the harm. That implies enforcement parity, entity-level consequences (fines, probation, suspension, oversight, dissolution where appropriate), and accountability for the people in charge: officers and boards should face criminal liability when the company commits serious crimes on their watch. It also implies aligning rights with responsibilities—for example, restricting political spending by corporations that have been convicted of crimes, as a corporate analogue to felony disenfranchisement.

---

## 1. Civil and Constitutional Personhood

Corporations enjoy legal personhood for a wide range of purposes. They enter contracts, hold property, sue and are sued in tort. They have constitutional rights: First Amendment protection for speech, including political spending (*Citizens United v. FEC*); Fourth Amendment and due process; right to counsel and jury trial; protection against excessive fines. They have limited liability and a separate juridical identity from shareholders and officers—they are, in law, *persons* for the purposes of holding rights and bearing civil liability (CRS R43293; *Citizens United v. FEC*).

That personhood is not conditional on good behavior. It is the default. The question this essay presses is whether the same person should be held to the same standard when it breaks the law (CRS R43293; *Citizens United v. FEC*, 558 U.S. 310 (2010)).

---

## 2. Criminal Liability Today

Under U.S. federal law, corporations *can* be criminally liable. A corporation is liable for federal crimes committed by employees or agents (1) within the scope of employment and (2) with intent to benefit the company (CRS R43293). The U.S. Sentencing Guidelines (Chapter 8) apply to organizations convicted of felonies and Class A misdemeanors. Penalties include fines, probation, restitution, forfeiture, and restrictions on commercial activity. Corporations cannot be jailed; the entity has no body to incarcerate (USSC Ch. 8; DOJ Justice Manual 9-28.000).

In practice, enforcement is highly discretionary. The DOJ decides whether to prosecute; factors include seriousness, corporate history, compliance programs, and cooperation. Many matters are resolved through deferred prosecution agreements (DPAs) or guilty pleas rather than trial (CRS R43293; DOJ Principles). Individuals—officers and employees—remain separately liable for their own crimes, but corporate prosecution does not substitute for individual accountability; the two can and should run in parallel. The gap is that corporate criminal enforcement is often perceived and practiced as a softer track: fines and agreements rather than the full weight of the criminal law.

---

## 3. The Asymmetry

There is a persistent asymmetry. **Civil:** Corporations are routinely persons for rights (speech, property, due process) and for civil liability (sued, fined by regulators). **Criminal:** Corporations can be prosecuted and punished, but in practice enforcement is discretionary, resolutions are often settlements, there is no incarceration for the entity, and corporate crime is often treated in public and policy discourse as less grave than street crime—an echo of the white-collar parity problem (Coffee, "Crime and the Corporation: Making the Punishment Fit the Corporation").

The logical claim is symmetry. If the corporation is a person for rights and civil duties, it should be a person for criminal duties too: investigated and punished with the same seriousness as natural persons for analogous harm, within the limits of what can be done to an entity. No jail, but fines, probation, suspension, receivership, or dissolution where the violation and history warrant. The "person" that enjoys First Amendment and property rights should bear full criminal accountability when it commits serious crimes.

---

## 4. Officers and Board: Liability on Their Watch

The entity cannot be jailed, but the people who run it can. A second dimension of symmetry is **officer and board liability** when the company commits serious crimes on their watch.

Current law already recognizes some of this. Officers are liable when they personally commit, direct, aid/abet, or conspire in the crime (CRS R43293). In addition, the **Responsible Corporate Officer (RCO)** doctrine—*United States v. Dotterweich*, 320 U.S. 277 (1943), *United States v. Park*, 421 U.S. 658 (1975)—allows conviction of executives in certain regulatory contexts (e.g. Federal Food, Drug and Cosmetic Act) **without** proof of personal intent or knowledge. The elements are: (1) position of responsibility and authority, (2) duty to prevent violations and implement measures so they do not occur, (3) "responsible relation to the situation." So "on their watch" liability already exists in narrow, strict-liability settings.

The **gap** is that for most federal crimes—fraud, bribery, environmental crime, platform or audit violations—prosecutors must prove the officer's own mens rea or direct participation. There is no general rule that the CEO, COO, or board are criminally liable simply because the company committed a serious crime and they had the authority to prevent or stop it.

The policy direction is to **extend RCO-style or recklessness-based liability** to more categories of corporate crime. When the company commits a serious crime and senior officers or the board had authority to prevent it and failed to take reasonable steps—or recklessly disregarded known risks—they should face criminal liability. The entity pays fines, probation, or suspension; the people in charge face the possibility of incarceration. That makes accountability personal as well as corporate and aligns with the idea that corporations as persons cannot be the only ones "punished"—the humans who run them must be accountable too.

## 5. One-Pager and Platform Accountability

This project's verification and audit one-pager proposes a regime in which platforms have a verification duty, face independent audit, and incur penalties for fake accounts and coordinated or state-run campaigns—with escalation to "three strikes" and then suspension or placement under an independent entity (dx/verification-and-audit-penalty-one-pager.md).

That regime is a **serious, person-like consequence** for the corporate person: loss of liberty to operate, or temporary loss of control—analogous in severity to felony-level consequences for individuals. Framing the max penalty (suspension or oversight) as a serious violation, and three strikes as warranting it, aligns with the principle that corporations as persons should face criminal or quasi-criminal consequences when they repeatedly or egregiously break the rules. One can go further: define certain **willful or repeated platform violations**—e.g. knowingly tolerating state-run campaigns, or falsifying audit results—as **criminal offenses** for the corporation, with fines and/or court-ordered oversight (receiver, monitor) as the sanction. Then the corporate person is not only civilly or administratively penalized but **criminally** accountable.

## 6. Citizens United and Symmetry

*Citizens United* held that corporations have First Amendment rights to spend on political campaigns. If corporations have those rights, they should bear **corresponding civic and criminal responsibility** when they misuse the information environment—e.g. by hosting state-run campaigns, systemic inauthenticity, or other violations that undermine democratic discourse. Rights and responsibilities should be aligned.

One concrete proposal is **corporate disenfranchisement**: restrict or bar political spending by corporations that have been **convicted of crimes**, mirroring the collateral consequence of felony disenfranchisement for natural persons. The same "person" that has speech rights would then bear a consequence after criminal conviction. This has been proposed in the academic literature (e.g. Michigan Business & Entrepreneurial Law Review, "Crafting a Corporate Analogue to Criminal Disenfranchisement"); no jurisdiction has enacted it yet (dx/corporations-as-persons.md). It could be implemented by statute and would likely face a First Amendment challenge; the reply is that content-neutral restrictions tied to criminal conviction, applied to the same entity that enjoys the right, are a permissible alignment of personhood—rights and responsibilities together (*Citizens United v. FEC*).

---

## 7. Conclusion and Policy Implications

Corporations are persons for rights and for civil liability. They should be persons for criminal responsibility too: same seriousness in enforcement, consequences that fit the entity, and accountability for the humans in charge.

**Policy implications:**

1. **Enforcement parity** — Treat corporate crime with the same investigative and prosecutorial seriousness as analogous harm by natural persons; reduce reliance on DPAs where a criminal conviction and sentencing would be more appropriate.

2. **Entity-level consequences** — Use the full range of Ch. 8 and statutory options: fines, probation, restitution, forfeiture, suspension of activities, court-ordered oversight (monitor, receiver), and in the most egregious or repeated cases, dissolution or equivalent. Three-strikes-style escalation (as in the verification one-pager) fits this.

3. **Officer and board liability** — Extend RCO-style or recklessness-based criminal liability to more categories of corporate crime so that CEOs, COOs, and boards face prosecution when the company commits serious crimes and they had authority to prevent or stop it.

4. **Corporate disenfranchisement** — Enact statutes that restrict or bar political spending by corporations convicted of crimes, as a corporate analogue to felony disenfranchisement, so that the same person that has speech rights bears collateral consequences after conviction.

Objections can be anticipated. "Punishing the corporation hurts workers and shareholders"—fines and probation can be designed with restitution and compliance; officer liability targets decision-makers; suspension is for repeat or egregious cases. "Officer liability will chill risk-taking"—the standard can be recklessness or known failure to prevent, not strict liability for all crimes. "Corporate disenfranchisement violates the First Amendment"—the reply is symmetry: same person, same responsibilities, content-neutral restriction tied to conviction.

Corporations as persons is not only a doctrinal category; it is a commitment to holding the same legal persons to one standard of accountability. The next step is to make that standard real in enforcement, sentencing, and the liability of the people who run them.
